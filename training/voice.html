Master Plan/ Training/Voice

Voice

The largest subsection — pitch, resonance, intonation, integration

Voice is the skill most often identified as the difference between being read correctly or not. It's trainable — that's well established. The work is slow and needs to be done consistently over months to years. Progress isn't linear; plateaus are normal and don't mean failure.
Overview

What voice training actually is

Two separate skills that people conflate: pitch (how high or low the voice sits) and resonance (where in the body the voice vibrates). Pitch alone doesn't feminize a voice — a high-pitched but chest-resonant voice reads as a high-pitched male voice. Resonance matters as much or more than pitch. Real work involves both, plus intonation (the musical shape of sentences) and integration (holding the voice under emotional or fatigue stress).

Honest framing: voice training typically shows first results in weeks, working stability in months, and genuine integration in a year or two. This is a long skill, not a sprint.

Prerequisites

Before starting

  • Private space to practice. Bedroom with door closed is enough. Voice training is vulnerable — doing it where you can be overheard defeats the point.
  • Something to record on. Phone's voice memo app is fine. Self-recording is how you hear your voice from outside; the in-head version is unreliable.
  • No pre-existing vocal damage or pain. If the voice feels strained, stop. Voice training should never hurt. Hoarseness, pain, or loss of voice after practice means something is being done wrong.
  • Accept the bad recordings. Early recordings will sound terrible to your own ears. Listen anyway. The ear gets calibrated only through hearing yourself.
Stage framework

The five-stage progression

This framework maps across all training subsections, not just voice. Milestones within each subsection are measured against these stages.

  • Introduced You're aware of it, have tried it once or twice, can't reliably do it.
  • Deliberate You can do it when focused, but it requires active attention. Drops the moment you stop thinking about it.
  • Supported You can do it in controlled or safe contexts consistently — home, with a familiar person, in low-stress situations.
  • Default It happens without conscious thought in low-stakes situations.
  • Integrated Stable across high-stakes, fatigued, or emotional contexts. The real finish line.
Milestones

Voice-specific milestone ladder

  1. Foundation — higher pitch on command
    Can produce a higher pitch when asked, even briefly. Doesn't need to sustain. Doesn't need to sound natural yet. Just accessible. P1–2
  2. Control — sustain through a full sentence
    Can hold the higher pitch through an entire sentence without dropping back at the end. Reading aloud is easier than conversation at this stage; both count. P2–3
  3. Resonance — forward placement
    The voice sits in a smaller, more forward space in the face rather than chest. This is where the feminine quality actually lives; pitch alone is not enough. P3–4
  4. Intonation — natural musical shape
    Upward lilt at phrase ends, broader pitch range within sentences, varied rhythm. Masculine speech patterns tend to be flatter; feminine patterns are more melodic. P3–4
  5. Integration — controlled environments
    Passing on phone calls, in short interactions with strangers, during coffee orders. Controlled stakes, short duration. The first time the voice works in public. P4–5
  6. Sustain — full conversation without fatigue
    Can hold the voice for a long conversation without the voice slipping back or becoming tired. Voice endurance — similar to how a runner builds mileage. P5
  7. Emotional range — voice works under stress
    Voice holds while laughing, arguing, being excited, being angry, being sad. The hardest stage because emotion accesses older patterns. P5–6
  8. Default — automatic without conscious effort
    The voice comes out without thinking about it. Old patterns might still emerge under extreme stress or exhaustion, but normal operation is automatic. P6
Practice structure

How often, how long, in what form

  • Frequency: short daily is better than long occasional. 10–15 minutes a day beats an hour once a week.
  • Format: read aloud, then record, then listen back. Record → listen → adjust is the loop; recording alone isn't practice.
  • Environment: alone, private, not when tired (fatigue blocks access to new patterns).
  • What to read: varied material. Neutral text (news articles), dialogue (plays, novels with conversation), technical material (where you'd normally revert to "work voice").
  • Conversation practice: separate from reading practice. Solo conversation (talking to yourself, narrating tasks) is the bridge between reading and actual conversation.
  • Rest the voice when needed. If it's tired or strained, stop. Push through = damage, not progress.
Resources

Where to learn — tiered

Free

  • TransVoiceLessons on YouTube (Zheanna Erose) — the most comprehensive free resource. Start here. Lessons on pitch, resonance, intonation, and the distinction between them. Specifically designed for trans femme voices.
  • Selene iO (YouTube) — similar space, different voice. Worth it for alternate explanations if TransVoiceLessons' framing isn't clicking.
  • TG-related subreddits (r/transvoice) — community feedback on recordings. Uneven in quality but free and interactive.

Paid (software)

  • Christella VoiceUp app — structured exercises, tracks pitch. ~$10 one-time or similar.
  • Voice Tools / Voice Pitch Analyzer apps — free or cheap, help with pitch feedback in real time. Phone apps.

Professional

  • Speech-language pathologist (SLP) with trans voice experience — the gold standard. Some offer remote sessions. Look for "gender-affirming voice therapy" as a specialty. Typical rates $80–200/session.
  • TransVoiceLessons 1:1 sessions — direct from Zheanna. Waitlisted, expensive, worth it for serious work.
  • Your insurance may cover SLP visits — worth checking, especially post-HRT access when medical infrastructure is more defined.
Professional help is worth real investment at the right stage Free resources are enough to build through the first few milestones. Around the "resonance" and "intonation" stages (milestones 3–4), an SLP with trans voice experience can compress months of trial-and-error into weeks of targeted work. Budget a few sessions in Phase 4–5 if possible.
