Posture & Movement

Standing, walking, sitting, transitions, the things bodies do constantly

The body is visible all the time. Posture and movement patterns are harder to consciously control than most other training categories because they run in the background — you're walking without thinking about walking. Training them means retraining the defaults, which is slow but real work.
Overview

What this subsection covers

Patterns of how the body holds itself at rest and in motion. Standing posture, walking mechanics, sitting positions, transitions between postures, how things are carried. Each is a small detail; collectively they're the biggest nonverbal signal of gender next to voice.

These are built over childhood and adolescence in most people. Retraining them as an adult requires conscious attention until new patterns become automatic — typically months to years, with voice-training-like plateaus.

Prerequisites

Before starting

  • A mirror or reflective surface — full length if possible. Movement and posture need visual feedback.
  • Video recording — phone on a shelf works. The view of yourself walking away from a camera is essential; you don't see your own back.
  • Willingness to feel awkward — new patterns feel affected before they feel natural. Push through the initial strangeness.
Areas

Standing

  • Hip shift awareness. Feminine standing often has weight on one hip rather than evenly distributed on both. The shifted hip creates the curve at the waist.
  • Weight distribution. Slightly forward on the balls of the feet rather than back on the heels.
  • Elbow position — close to the torso. Women tend to stand and move with elbows held close to the sides of the ribs at rest. Men tend to hold elbows slightly away (partly structural, partly "taking space"). Elbows close to the body is the most overlooked posture adjustment and one of the most visible.
  • Shoulder position. Relaxed and slightly back rather than hunched forward.
  • Hand position. Softer rather than rigid. Hands clasped loosely in front, one hand on the hip, or at the sides with slight finger curl.

Walking

  • Narrower foot placement. Feet land closer to an imaginary center line rather than two parallel tracks. This creates the slight hip sway without needing to exaggerate it.
  • Hip engagement. Lead from the hips, not the feet. The hip goes forward first, the foot follows.
  • Smaller steps. Shorter stride length. Partly a function of the narrower foot placement; partly deliberate.
  • Arm swing. Less exaggerated, elbows stay closer to the body during the swing.
  • Shoulders still. Masculine walks often have shoulder rotation with each step; feminine walks keep the shoulders relatively square while the hips do the work.

Sitting

  • Knees together or crossed. Not splayed. This is the single most commented-on sitting difference.
  • Back straight. No slouching, but not rigid either — supported by the spine, not by the chair back necessarily.
  • Hand placement. Hands in lap, or on the table, or one hand on each thigh — rarely both hanging at sides.
  • Cross-legged sitting. Top leg crossed over at the knee, foot of top leg angled toward the floor. The "ankle on knee" crossed-leg position is coded masculine.

Transitions

  • Standing up from seated. Feet together under the chair, hands on thighs or chair arms to push up, stand smoothly. Avoid the push-off-with-both-hands maneuver.
  • Sitting down. Smooth descent, knees together especially if in a skirt.
  • Getting in and out of cars. Knees together, swing both legs simultaneously (in or out), pivot on the seat. This is especially important in skirts; practice in pants first.
  • Turning around. Pivot on the balls of the feet with a smaller radius rather than a wide step-around.

Carrying

  • Bags. Over the shoulder with hand on the strap, in the crook of the elbow (more formal), or held by the top handle. Not across the body unless specifically styled that way.
  • Phones. Held with fingers on the back and thumb on the screen; avoid the palm-down flat-hand grip which reads neutral-masculine.
  • Objects in general. Held closer to the body rather than at arm's length.
Resources

Where to learn

Free

  • YouTube: "feminine body language" and "feminine walking" searches — mixed quality; filter for channels with actual instruction rather than promotional content.
  • Observational practice. Watch cis women in public (non-creepily — notice patterns, not individuals). Coffee shops, restaurants, workplaces. The patterns become visible once you're looking for them.
  • Video of style icons. Ana de Armas, Ariana Grande, France Gall, Princess Catherine all have extensive video footage. Their movement patterns are reference material.

Paid

  • Dance or movement classes — ballet, burlesque, contemporary. Classes retrain body awareness broadly in ways that apply directly. Local studios; typical drop-in ~$20.
  • Etiquette coaching — exists as a category for business-adjacent audiences but transfers well. Can be expensive.
  • Physical therapist — if specific mobility limitations are in the way (a common issue from prolonged sitting), a few PT sessions to address hip flexors and pelvic tilt have practical benefits beyond presentation.
Practice structure

How to work on this

  • Pick one pattern per week. Not all of them at once. Standing elbow position this week. Walking foot placement next week. The pattern becomes automatic only when it's been the focus.
  • Mirror work daily. A few minutes in front of the mirror — stand, shift weight, sit, stand, turn. Notice what the body defaults to.
  • Record walking weekly. Phone on a shelf, walk past the camera a few times. Watch the footage; compare to reference video.
  • At home first, outside second. New patterns feel too conspicuous to try in public before they feel possible at home.
Milestones

Using the five-stage framework

Each of the pattern areas moves through Introduced → Deliberate → Supported → Default → Integrated independently. Typical rough pacing:

  • Phase 1–2 — Standing (hip shift, elbow position). Start here; easiest to work on at home.
  • Phase 2–4 — Walking (foot placement, hip engagement, arm swing). Gradually public.
  • Phase 3–4 — Sitting and transitions. Integrated into daily life.
  • Phase 4–5 — Carrying and fine motor patterns. The last details.
  • Phase 6 — All areas at Default or Integrated.
