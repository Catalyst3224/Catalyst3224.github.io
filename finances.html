Finances

The pacing that makes everything else sustainable

Financial milestones are the spine of the phased plan. Every other section's purchasing pace is calibrated to where this section is. Debt paydown first, savings second, then Columbus/OSU transition, then independent living. The money work isn't flashy but it's what lets everything else actually happen.
Goal

What this section is doing

Not a comprehensive personal finance plan — just the specific milestones that gate the other sections. Debt free → emergency buffer → grad school transition fund → independent living. Each milestone unlocks more discretionary capacity; each phase of the plan is calibrated to the financial state it assumes.

Phase detail

Phase 1–2: Debt paydown

  • $200 biweekly on Card 1
  • $100 biweekly on Card 2
  • No new debt
  • Projected debt free by Month 5–6
  • Any unexpected income (tax refund, etc.) → debt first, not savings yet

Phase 2–3: Savings launch

  • Cards paid off or nearly so by end of Phase 2
  • Redirect $300 biweekly toward savings
  • Target: $1k savings milestone by Month 7–8
  • This is the first point where meaningful wardrobe or personal care investment becomes reasonable

Phase 3–4: Building toward $5k

  • Continue building toward $5k
  • Research Columbus cost of living and OSU grad school program
  • Confirm GI Bill access and calculate housing allowance for Columbus zip codes
  • Check credit score — know where you stand before any future rental application
  • Research OSU RF engineering program — application deadlines, requirements, GRE if needed
  • Begin understanding what a rental application requires

Phase 4–5: Transition groundwork

  • Savings growing toward $5k
  • GI Bill paperwork in motion
  • First wig purchase timing aligns here (see Wigs)
  • More intentional clothing investment becomes reasonable

Phase 5–6: Columbus move

  • $5k savings milestone achieved
  • GI Bill activated or in process
  • Columbus move planning concrete — timeline, logistics, budget
  • Housing allowance calculated into grad school financial plan
  • Begin building emergency fund concept — separate from savings goal (at least one month's expenses, ideally three)

Phase 6: Independent living

  • Own space (rental in Columbus or wherever grad school lands)
  • No discretion constraints unless you choose them
  • Build life entirely on your own terms
  • Longer horizon: Seattle or PNW when the timing is right
Practical notes

Operational guidance

  • Savings account separate from checking — at a different bank if possible. Friction reduces the temptation to spend what's saved.
  • Automatic transfers — set up on payday rather than manual at month end. What you never see in checking, you don't spend.
  • Budget in categories, not line items — "personal care" as a monthly bucket, not tracked per-purchase. Fidelity costs more in mental overhead than it saves in dollars.
  • Emergency fund is separate — once $5k savings is hit, the next goal is a genuine emergency fund (3 months of expenses), separate from any transition fund. Don't conflate them.
  • Credit score maintenance — keep the cards open after payoff (closing cards hurts score). Use one small recurring charge on each, auto-pay in full.
GI Bill

The specific lever

The GI Bill is the mechanism that makes Columbus/OSU financially feasible. Key items to confirm:

  • Eligibility — Post-9/11 GI Bill based on service history. 100% tier based on length of qualifying service.
  • Housing allowance — BAH-based, varies by zip code and enrollment status. Columbus zip codes are the relevant ones to calculate.
  • Book stipend — additional annual amount for course materials.
  • Yellow Ribbon Program — supplemental funding for private or out-of-state tuition; less relevant for OSU as in-state public but worth knowing.

A veteran-specialized academic advisor should be the first real conversation for getting these concrete — military credits applied, CLEP strategy, which courses count toward prerequisites vs. general electives. The advisor conversation is a Phase 4 priority at the latest.

Integration

How this section paces everything else

Every other section's purchasing decisions are gated by this one. Wardrobe investment scales with financial phase. Wig purchases align with $1k savings. Medical/HRT care has its own cost stream that needs to fit within the phased capacity. The rule across sections is quality over quantity, timed to financial state — the plan prevents the common failure mode of buying everything at once, then being unable to maintain the routines because money ran out.
