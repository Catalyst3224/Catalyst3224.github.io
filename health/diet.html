Diet

Principles that support fitness and HRT — not a whole project

No calorie counting, no macro tracking, no elimination protocols. The goal is directional: adequate protein, enough overall food to support training, minimal friction. A diet this section turns into a whole project is a diet that stops being done.
Goal

What this is trying to do

Support two things the body is actively doing — building muscle from the fitness plan, undergoing HRT-driven fat redistribution — without generating the kind of food stress that creates mental health costs. The honest frame is: eat enough, eat with protein, don't fight the body. Precision is not the goal.

Principles

The short version

  • Protein at most meals. Eggs, chicken, fish, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, legumes. Enough to support the training adaptation — a rough target of palm-sized portion at 2–3 meals per day.
  • Vegetables daily. Whatever's tolerable and available. Frozen is as good as fresh for nutrition; convenience matters.
  • Fiber. Whole grains, beans, fruit, vegetables. Digestion runs better when fiber is present.
  • Hydration. Water through the day; more on training days.
  • Don't train fasted if strength is the priority — eat something 60–90 min before lifting.
What to avoid as a framework

Friction, not food

  • Calorie counting unless a specific stall justifies it. The accuracy isn't worth the mental overhead for most goals.
  • Elimination diets (keto, paleo, carnivore) — none of these have evidence for body recomposition outcomes beyond what standard adequate protein + training already produces.
  • Treating "clean" and "dirty" foods as moral categories. They aren't. A burger is fine. An ice cream is fine.
HRT-specific notes

A few things worth knowing

  • Soy — the long-running "phytoestrogen" concern has mostly been debunked in clinical research. Soy is fine. It doesn't replace or meaningfully interfere with HRT dosing.
  • Flaxseed, cruciferous vegetables — same story. Food-level amounts of "estrogen-adjacent" compounds aren't dose-relevant compared to pharmaceutical HRT.
  • Calcium, vitamin D, bone support — becomes more relevant with HRT, especially long-term. Supplements cover this (see Supplements); dietary contribution from dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens is additive.
  • Salt and water retention — some HRT regimens increase water retention mildly. Not a reason to cut salt dramatically unless specifically advised by a provider.
Mental health intersection

Keep this out of disordered territory

Food is one of the easier places for disordered thinking to take hold, especially during a period of active body change. The goal of this section is explicitly not to optimize. If tracking or restriction starts feeling compulsive, loosen it rather than tightening it. Weight on the scale is a noisy signal during recomposition and HRT; how clothes fit and how the body feels are better indicators.

If food thinking starts taking up too much space Step away from any tracking. Stop weighing. Eat at normal times, with normal portions, without much planning. The diet section is not a place to move closer to disordered patterns — it's a place to stay loosely oriented while the rest of the plan does its work.
Resources

When more detail is wanted

  • Renaissance Periodization / Mike Israetel — reasonable evidence-based framing for protein and training-adjacent nutrition without being bro-science.
  • Examine.com — neutral research database for supplement and dietary questions.
  • Registered dietitian — if a specific goal (e.g. athletic performance, medical condition) requires more structure, an RD visit is worth the cost. GI Bill may cover this through future education; current employer benefits may also.
