Fitness

Three days per week · Full body · Dumbbells and resistance bands

Fat loss and muscle building simultaneously — body recomposition. Specific emphasis on gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, and hip flexors. Gradual, sustainable progression that respects asthma limitations.
Goal

What the program is for

Three non-consecutive days per week — Monday/Wednesday/Friday or similar. After roughly six years of detraining, a full-body split makes more sense than a body-part split: everything responds to everything, and missing a day isn't a disaster because you haven't skipped an entire muscle group for the week. Primary goals are body recomposition (fat loss and muscle gain simultaneously), increased resting metabolic rate through muscle development, and specific emphasis on glutes and hip flexors — both aesthetic and functional given the seated-work history.

Asthma note Always have your inhaler accessible during workouts. Ramp intensity gradually — never push through breathing distress. Block 1 compounds will elevate heart rate significantly; take longer rests between sets if needed rather than reducing weight. Swimming is an excellent asthma-friendly cardio option if accessible later.
Theory

Why full-body over a split

Splits — push/pull/legs, upper/lower, bro-splits — work when you're training five or more days per week and need to fit everything in without overtraining any single group. At three sessions per week the math flips. A full-body session trains every major muscle group three times weekly; a split at three days hits each group once, which isn't enough to drive adaptation in a previously-untrained body.

The structure each session follows: short warmup, Block 1 (compound movements — big lifts that recruit lots of muscle), Block 2 (glute/hip focus — the emphasis area), Block 3 (core — stability work once warm), brief static stretching, walk cooldown.

Session structure

The pattern for every session

  1. Warmup — 5 min easy walking + dynamic mobility (leg swings, arm circles, bodyweight squats)
  2. Block 1: Compound movements — ~20 min
  3. Block 2: Glutes & hip flexors — ~15 min
  4. Block 3: Core — ~5 min
  5. Post-workout stretching — 5–10 min static holds on worked muscles (30–60s each)
  6. Cooldown walk — 15–20 min moderate walking
Block 1 — Compound movements

The biggest-impact lifts

Heavy, multi-joint movements that recruit the most muscle and drive metabolic demand. Always performed first when the body is fresh.

01Goblet Squat 3 × 10–12
  • Hold one dumbbell vertically at chest height
  • Feet shoulder-width apart, toes slightly out
  • Squat deep — hips below parallel if mobility allows
  • Drive through heels to stand, squeeze glutes at top
Quadriceps · Gluteus maximus · Core
02Romanian Deadlift (RDL) 3 × 10–12
  • Hold dumbbells in front of thighs, slight bend in knees
  • Hinge at hips, push them back, lower weights along legs
  • Feel the hamstring stretch — go until form breaks, not further
  • Drive hips forward to return, squeeze glutes at top
Hamstrings · Gluteus maximus · Lower back
03Dumbbell Row 3 × 10–12 each side
  • Brace one hand and knee on a surface for support
  • Pull dumbbell to hip, lead with elbow not hand
  • Full stretch at bottom, full contraction at top
Lats · Rhomboids · Biceps · Rear deltoid
04Dumbbell Floor Press or Incline Press 3 × 10–12
  • Lie on floor (or incline surface), dumbbells at chest
  • Press up and slightly in, don't lock elbows fully
  • Control the descent — 3 seconds down, press up
Pectorals · Anterior deltoid · Triceps
05Dumbbell Overhead Press 2–3 × 10
  • Seated or standing, dumbbells at shoulder height
  • Press directly overhead, avoid excessive back arch
  • Lower with control to shoulder height
Deltoids · Triceps · Upper traps
Block 2 — Glute & hip flexor focus

The emphasis area

This block directly targets the specific development goals. Do not skip or rush this section — it's where the aesthetic emphasis and functional correction lives. The full resistance band set makes this block possible at home.

06Resistance Band Glute Bridge 3 × 15
  • Loop band just above knees, lie on back, feet flat
  • Drive knees out against band throughout movement
  • Press hips to ceiling, hold 2 seconds at top, squeeze hard
  • Lower slowly — 3 seconds down
Gluteus maximus · Gluteus medius (via band)
07Resistance Band Lateral Walk 3 × each direction
  • Band just above knees (or around ankles for harder)
  • Slight squat position, stay low throughout
  • Step sideways 10–12 steps each direction = 1 set
  • Keep hips level — don't hike one side up
Gluteus medius · TFL · Hip abductors
08Reverse Lunge with Knee Drive 3 × 10 each leg
  • Step back into lunge, lower rear knee toward floor
  • Drive forward knee up toward chest as you return
  • Control the movement — no momentum
Gluteus maximus · Hip flexors · Quadriceps
09Standing Hip Flexor March (Band) 3 × 12 each side
  • Anchor band low or loop around foot and fixed point
  • Drive knee up against resistance to hip height
  • Hold 1 second at top, lower with control
Iliopsoas · Rectus femoris (hip flexors)
10Side-Lying Clamshell 3 × 15 each side
  • Band just above knees, lie on side, hips stacked
  • Feet together, rotate top knee up like a clamshell opening
  • Hold 2 seconds at top, lower slowly
  • Do not let hips roll backward — keep them stacked
Gluteus medius · External hip rotators
Block 3 — Core

Stability rather than crunches

Core work at the end when the body is warm. Focus on stability and control rather than crunches or sit-ups.

11Dead Bug 3 × 8–10 each side
  • Lie on back, arms pointing to ceiling, knees at 90°
  • Slowly lower opposite arm and leg toward floor
  • Keep lower back pressed into floor throughout
  • Return and repeat other side = 1 rep
Deep core stabilizers · Transverse abdominis
12Pallof Press (Band) 3 × 10 each side
  • Anchor band at chest height to side, stand perpendicular
  • Hold band at chest, press straight out and hold 2 seconds
  • Resist the rotation — the anti-rotation is the exercise
  • Return to chest slowly
Obliques · Core stabilizers
13Plank 2–3 sets
  • Forearms or straight arms, body in a straight line
  • Don't let hips sag or pike up
  • Build from 20–30 seconds toward 60+ seconds over time
Full core · Shoulder stabilizers
Mobility & flexibility

Three layers, each with a different purpose

Flexibility work fills gaps the strength plan doesn't address: maintaining range of motion under load, protecting against injury, improving recovery, and opening up the hip flexors and thoracic spine that chronic sitting shortens.

Daily mobility — 5 minutes, morning

Woven into the morning personal care block. Not a formal session — just a brief movement routine to wake the body up and reset the nervous system.

  • Cat-cow — 10 reps, warms the spine
  • World's greatest stretch — 5 each side, opens hips and thoracic spine
  • Standing hip flexor stretch — 30 seconds each side
  • Thoracic rotation — 10 each side
  • Standing forward fold — 30 seconds

Post-workout stretching — 5–10 minutes

After Block 3 core work, before the walk cooldown. Static holds on the muscles just trained. 30–60 seconds per stretch, deep enough to feel but never into pain.

  • Standing or seated forward fold — hamstrings (after RDLs and squats)
  • Kneeling hip flexor stretch — hip flexors (after lunges)
  • Doorway chest stretch — pecs and anterior shoulder (after press work)
  • Figure-4 lying on back — glutes and piriformis
  • Cross-body shoulder stretch — rear deltoid
  • Child's pose with side reaches — lats and lower back

Weekly longer session — 20–60 minutes

Handled via the 90-day yoga bootcamp already owned. Slot sessions on rest days (Tuesday, Thursday) or weekend mornings.

  • Tuesday or Thursday evening — lower-intensity sessions (yin, restorative, gentle flow)
  • Saturday or Sunday morning — any intensity, longer sessions fine
  • Skip or modify if a session repeats poses that cause actual pain (not discomfort) — programs sometimes assume baseline flexibility you may not have
  • Treat the 90 days as "the next session whenever you yoga next" rather than 90 consecutive days — missing days is normal; the program still progresses

Yoga vs. pilates

Both are valuable but serve different functions. Yoga prioritizes flexibility, mobility, and breath work — the bigger gap given current training history. Pilates is more core-strength focused and closer to what Block 3 already does. Yoga is the better weekly-session addition; pilates can come later if the interest is there.

Timing notes

  • Dynamic stretching before lifting (movement through range) — leg swings, arm circles, bodyweight squats as part of the warmup
  • Static stretching after lifting or on rest days — never before, as it reduces force production
  • Breath-focused stretching helps asthma symptoms over time — slow nasal breathing during holds trains the diaphragm
Progression

When to level up

The body adapts quickly early on. Follow these rules to keep progressing:

  • General rule: when you can complete all sets and reps with good form and feel like you had 2–3 reps left in the tank, increase the weight slightly (2.5–5 lb) on the next session.
  • Never compromise form for weight. A heavier lift done poorly is worse than a lighter lift done well.
  • Plateaus are information, not failure. If a movement stalls for 2–3 weeks, try swapping rep range (8 instead of 12), changing tempo, or adding a pause.
  • Deload weeks every 6–8 weeks: cut weight by 40%, maintain reps. Feels too easy on paper, crucial for recovery.
Cardio & walking

Walking is doing work

The walking you're already doing is valuable and should continue. Don't replace it with gym sessions — they're additive, not alternative.

  • Continue daily walking as a baseline
  • The 15–20 minute post-session walk counts toward daily steps
  • On rest days, walking is encouraged — active recovery beats full inactivity
  • Avoid high-intensity cardio (running, HIIT) until breathing capacity is well established — several months minimum
  • Swimming is an excellent asthma-friendly option if accessible later
Nutrition integration

Simple framing

Not tracked currently and doesn't need to be. Full treatment on the Diet page. The relevant fitness-adjacent notes:

  • Protein at most meals — eggs, chicken, fish, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, legumes
  • Hydration before, during, after
  • Don't train fasted if strength is the priority — eat something 60–90 min before
