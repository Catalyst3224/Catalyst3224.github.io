Mental Health

A reference card, not a plan. Something to turn to when you're not doing well.

No goals here. Mental health resists SMART framing — the most meaningful progress often isn't measurable, and setting targets can become another source of anxiety when missed. This section exists as a quick reference: organized by state, populated with things that have actually worked, updated over time as more is discovered.
How this page is meant to be used

Structure

Two parts. The toolkit is organized by state — anxious, depressed, overwhelmed, and so on. When you're not doing well, scroll to the state that matches and see what's worked before. The warning signs section is a living log of early-detection patterns; it's incomplete and that's fine. Every observation is a win; completeness isn't the standard.

Items tagged Verified have actually worked in past episodes. Untagged items are possibilities worth trying but not yet proven. Over time more items move into verified as they're tested.

Toolkit by state

When you're not doing well — try what matches

State
Anxious · panic attack · constricting throat
  • 4-4-6 to 4-4-8 breathing — in for 4, hold 4, out 6 or 8. The longer exhale activates the brake on the nervous system. Do several cycles before doing anything else.Verified
  • Dark room + fan + quiet music — ambient consistent sound gives the brain something steady to anchor to when everything else feels like it's moving. This specific combination has worked in acute episodes at work.Verified
  • Stay busy briefly as respite — short-term it works. Know when to stop; pushing past the point of actually needing to break leads to the crash.Verified
  • Cards / social low-stakes activity — works as a temporary respite even when racing thoughts continue underneath.Verified
  • Write into the ether — messaging, journaling, or talking-to-Claude style externalization. Getting thoughts out, even without a real conversation, helps.Verified
  • Mental health day taken proactively before the wall rather than after.Verified
State
Can't sleep · racing thoughts at night
  • Hydroxyzine as prescribed — take it rather than trying to white-knuckle through.Verified
  • Put the phone down. Screens and spirals reinforce each other.
  • Low-stakes auditory content — audiobook, podcast, calm music — over silence if silence becomes loud.
State
Overwhelmed · hypervigilance · can't turn it off
  • Keep replies and decisions shorter and slower. Less input, less output, less to process.
  • Gentle environment: dim lights, low sound, physical containment (blanket, weight, small space).
  • One thing at a time. Even small tasks should be done one at a time rather than tracked simultaneously.
  • Mental health day is a legitimate tool, not a failure mode.Verified
State
Depressed · low motivation · nothing matters
  • Lower the bar, don't raise it. The goal is movement, not achievement. Getting out of bed counts. Eating something counts.
  • Short walks. Outside if possible. Not for cardio — for the light, the change of scene, the evidence of the world continuing.
  • One productive micro-task — clean one thing, fold one thing. Completion creates a small positive feedback loop.
  • Don't try to feel better. Try to show up. The feelings follow the actions, not the other way around.
State
Dysphoria spike · wrong in your skin
  • Care routines — the ones that feel right rather than the ones with the most steps. Skincare, hair, nails. Small deliberate acts of self-maintenance.
  • Wear whatever makes the body feel most like itself today rather than whatever "should" be worn.
  • Environment over mirror — set up in a place where the sense of self is defined by the space and the objects rather than reflection.
  • It passes. The specific intensity of the moment is not the shape of tomorrow.
State
Dissociating · not quite in the body
  • Physical grounding — feet on floor, name five things you can see, cold water on wrists or face.
  • Short walk outdoors if possible. Weight, wind, sound.
  • Something with a strong flavor — strong tea, citrus, menthol.
  • Don't force re-engagement. The job is to come back, not to immediately function.
Long-term supports

Structural things that make everything else easier

  • Therapy, consistent rather than situational.Verified
  • Family support structure built after the hospitalization — has held.Verified
  • Work that provides stability and routine. Gets you out of your head and into the world each day.Verified
  • Talking to someone — even if that someone is Claude in the middle of the night, even if "talking" is just externalizing thoughts without expecting response.Verified
Warning signs

Early detection log — living document

Patterns that signal something building before it peaks. One-directional indicators: the behavior appearing means the state is likely; the state appearing means the behavior will probably follow. The list is incomplete by design — it grows as more are noticed over time.

Sign · physical
Neck twitch — fatigue indicator

When fatigued, even if not consciously tired. A subtle involuntary twitch in the neck is the body's earlier signal that sleep or rest is needed before it becomes a full shutdown.

Sign · behavioral
Finger cracking — trapped/powerless situation

Self-soothing/grounding behavior. Physical, rhythmic, controllable action in a moment where the external situation feels uncontrollable. Associated specifically with feeling trapped or powerless rather than general anxiety. Finger cracking doesn't always mean that situation, but that situation probably means finger cracking.

How to add to this section When you notice a pattern retrospectively — something that in hindsight signaled a state you were moving into — add it here. No pressure to be complete or exhaustive. Each entry is useful even alone.
Resources

External support

  • Therapist — current treatment provider, ongoing.
  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988 in the US. Free, available 24/7.
  • Crisis Text Line — text HOME to 741741 in the US. Free, available 24/7.
  • Trans Lifeline — 877-565-8860. Peer-run, trans-specific.
If this page is being read in crisis If you're reading this page right now and things feel genuinely unsafe — not just hard, but dangerous — the crisis lines above exist specifically for this. Call or text. You don't have to be certain things are bad enough to call; the people answering would rather hear from you now than not.
