Aesthetic Framework

The shape of what we're building toward, and the vocabulary for describing it

Literary romantic with old money restraint and a slightly melancholy, ethereal edge. Candlelit libraries, autumn afternoons, silk ribbons, wool coats, the feeling of a French film from the 1960s.
Goal

What the framework is for

A working vocabulary. The framework does two jobs: it describes a register — elegant-formal, sexy-casual, and so on — cleanly enough that an outfit, a figure, or a mood can be placed on it; and it names a synthesized aesthetic so purchasing decisions have a target to evaluate against. Neither job is possible without both halves: the dual axis gives the grid, the synthesis gives the location.

Theory

The dual-axis framework

High/low femininity as a single spectrum does too much work. It tries to describe formality and restraint at the same time, and those aren't the same variable — a sexy cocktail dress and a polo shirt at a country club are both some kind of not casual, but they're operating on different axes entirely.

The better framing treats elegance ↔ sexiness and formality ↔ casualness as two independent axes. Every outfit, every figure, every register sits somewhere on the grid rather than on a single line.

Things slide around on this grid rather than sitting at fixed points. A slip dress is elegant-formal alone in the evening; add a chunky cardigan and sneakers over it and it lands in elegant-casual. The same figure can range across several quadrants across a career — see the Style Icons placements. Outfits in the Lookbook are tagged with their quadrant so the grid becomes usable rather than theoretical.

What the framework doesn't capture Age register (youthful vs. matured), cultural register (Parisian vs. Tokyo vs. New England prep), price tier, seasonality. The axes are about mode, not about everything. A framework that explains everything explains nothing.
Synthesis

The synthesized direction

The center of gravity sits in the elegant-casual quadrant, with extensions upward into elegant-formal (the wool coat, the silk blouse, the Mary Janes) and occasional moves into sexy-formal for specific occasions (slip dress, heels, statement jewelry). Sexy-casual is available but rarely the daily register.

Said another way: the question the wardrobe keeps answering is refined without trying, considered without stiff, feminine without costume. The literary, old-money, and French-film references all point to the same thing — restraint that reads as intentional rather than timid.

Color direction

Cool undertones throughout

Undertone has been confirmed cool — blue-purple veins, silver reads better than gold, aesthetic preference for cool tones predates the framework. This translates directly into product and piece selection.

  • Foundation/concealer: rosy/cool or neutral. Avoid golden or warm-labeled.
  • Blush: cool pinks, mauves, soft berries. Avoid peach and coral.
  • Eyeshadow: lilac, taupe, cool brown, grey, rose, soft blue.
  • Highlight: silver-based or pearl. Not gold or champagne.
  • Lip: cool pink, mauve, berry. If red, blue-based rather than orange-based.
  • Jewelry: silver hardware throughout. Pearls work especially well.
  • Clothing palette: black, cream, ivory, charcoal, navy, forest green, burgundy, dusty rose, lavender. No warm browns, no terracotta, no mustard.

Inspo overview

Inspo figures across the grid

Full treatment is on the Style Icons page with dual-axis placements and takeaways. The figures currently in the inspo set span the grid rather than clustering in one place:

  • Amal Clooney, Eva Marie Saint — almost exclusively elegant-formal
  • Princess Catherine — elegant-formal default, elegant-casual when she steps down
  • Anne Hathaway — elegant-formal and sexy-formal; occasional elegant-casual
  • France Gall, Ariana Grande — elegant-casual home base, ranging freely
  • Ana de Armas — sexy-formal home base, comfortable elegant-formal

Hindash is explicitly not in the inspo set. Their technique is useful as a makeup reference (see Makeup), but the aesthetic itself isn't a personal target.
