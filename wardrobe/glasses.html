Master Plan/ Wardrobe/Glasses

Glasses

Frames sit on the face all day — they carry aesthetic weight

Glasses aren't neutral. They're one of the most prominent identity markers on a face, and small choices about shape, color, and material change what the rest of the outfit is doing. The goal isn't dramatic — it's intentional.
Goal

Direction within the aesthetic

Frame styles that fit the capsule direction read intentional and slightly intellectual without being trendy. The aesthetic favors timelessness over of-the-moment. Everything on this page assumes the literary-romantic, old-money-restraint direction established in the framework.

Optical frames

Shapes that work

  • Round metal (thin) — the Lennon/Ghandi shape in delicate gold, silver, or rose-gold metal. Literary and slightly bookish without committing fully to either feminine or masculine. References: Warby Parker "Beale," Moscot "Lemtosh" or "Miltzen."
  • Soft cat-eye (subtle, not exaggerated) — feminine but understated. The 1950s/60s secretary version, not the dramatic 1990s drag-queen version. Look for "kitten cat-eye" or "modified cat-eye." Krewe and Garrett Leight do this well.
  • Browline / clubmaster — androgynous and slightly academic. Top of the frame is bold, bottom is metal or rimless. Originally menswear but now worn by everyone. References: Persol, Ray-Ban Clubmaster.
  • Oval acetate — quiet, classical, doesn't commit either direction. Reference: Garrett Leight "Hampton."
  • Wayfarer (current) — androgynous classic, slightly more casual register. Still valid; see Wayfarer replacement discussion below.

What to avoid

  • Aviators — too pilot-y / casual for this aesthetic
  • Square chunky frames — too 2010s-tech-bro
  • Sport frames of any kind
  • Rimless — reads dated/boomer unless very intentionally styled
  • Bright colors or transparent neon
  • Anything with visible logos or hardware
Materials & color

Color and material guidance

  • Tortoiseshell — the classic. Works with everything, reads literary-academic.
  • Black acetate — sharper, more severe, more Parisian.
  • Gold or rose-gold metal — softer, more feminine.
  • Silver metal — cooler, more architectural. Fits the cool-tone palette particularly well.
  • Avoid: brown plastic, pure white, matte black.

Sunglasses

Sunglass shapes

Same aesthetic vocabulary, with a few category-specific additions.

  • Oversized round (Audrey Hepburn / Jackie O territory) — the classic feminine option. Search "oversized round" or "Jackie."
  • Cat-eye sunglasses — works the same way as the optical version.
  • Wayfarers — androgynous classic, never really goes out of style.
  • Persol 714 / 649 (the Steve McQueen frames) — heritage Italian, reads sophisticated and slightly retro.
  • Avoid: sport sunglasses, mirrored lenses, anything that belongs at a music festival, visible logos.

Lens color matters too. Brown/amber lenses are warmer; grey/green lenses are more neutral and pair better with the cool-tone palette overall. Classic dark grey or green lenses over tortoise-frame-with-amber-lens.

Current situation

Wayfarer replacement — near-term priority

Current glasses are Wayfarers in the discontinued "Havana" colorway (Persol's slightly darker tortoiseshell, named after Cuban cigar wrappers). They need replacing. Three directions:

  1. Stay in the same lane — another tortoiseshell Wayfarer or Wayfarer-adjacent frame. Lowest disruption, familiar silhouette. Ray-Ban Wayfarer Classic in Havana is the direct replacement; Warby Parker's "Welty" or "Hayes" are Wayfarer shapes in tortoise at lower prices.
  2. Slight shift toward feminine within the same family — Wayfarer with softer corners or subtle cat-eye lift. Warby Parker's "Esme" and "Eugene" are Wayfarer-shape with subtly more feminine angles. Reads almost identical at a glance, quietly pulls feminine.
  3. Pivot to a new shape entirely — round metal, cat-eye, or browline. Bigger visual change, glasses doing more aesthetic work.
Recommendation — slight shift within the Wayfarer family Glasses sit on the face all day and are highly prominent. Everything else is already changing (clothing, hair via wig eventually, body composition over time). A familiar-but-slightly-feminized frame lets the rest of the work do the heavy lifting without the glasses competing. The full pivot to round metal or cat-eye is a Phase 4–5 move.
Starting points

Two practical recommendations

  1. Optical: Warby Parker home try-on with a mix of styles — one round metal, one cat-eye, one browline, one Wayfarer-cat-eye hybrid. Free, no commitment. ~$100 when you decide.
  2. Sunglasses: Search "oversized round sunglasses" or "Jackie O sunglasses" on Sezane or & Other Stories for European versions in the $80–120 range. Or vintage / secondhand on eBay for true heritage frames at lower prices.
