Tops

Five interchangeable tops, from foundational turtleneck to statement silk blouse

The top layer does most of the register-shifting in the capsule. A charcoal turtleneck with a silk skirt reads Parisian; the same turtleneck with a cream pleated midi reads literary academic. Five tops are enough to cover the full range.

Piece 01Fine-Knit Turtleneck · Charcoal

The foundational layering piece. A long-sleeved pullover with a high folded neck. Fine-gauge means thin yarn and small tight stitches — a smooth, nearly jersey-like surface rather than visible cables or waffle texture. Skims the body: fitted enough to show silhouette, loose enough to not cling.

What to look for

  • Fiber: cashmere, merino wool, or cashmere-merino blend (avoid acrylic/polyester-dominant)
  • Described as "fine-gauge," "featherweight," or "tissue-weight"
  • Neck folds cleanly at mid-throat height, neither loose cowl nor stiff mock
  • Available in multiple colorways (black-only is a mild red flag — you can't evaluate construction in black photos)
Black vs. charcoal Charcoal is usually the better choice. Black photographs poorly, fades visibly over time, and mismatches with other "blacks" in an outfit. Charcoal holds its depth, layers with everything, and reads intentional rather than default. If a second turtleneck is ever added, black ribbed in a more fitted Parisian cut is the natural pair.

Piece 02Silk Blouse · Ivory

Long-sleeved top that drapes rather than stretches. Fluid, slightly loose through the body, with movement when you turn. Neckline options: notch collar (softer men's-shirt), pussy-bow (neck ties you can knot or leave loose), round neck, or split-neck pullover. The defining feature is drape — it should fall in soft folds, not hold its own shape.

What to look for

  • 100% silk (mulberry silk ideal) or silk-cotton blends. Washable silk is silk treated for machine washing — still real silk
  • Matte or subtle sheen, not high-gloss "wet look"
  • Buttons sit flat when closed; no pulling or gaping
  • Hem falls evenly; clean interior finishing (French seams ideal)
  • For cool undertones, ivory reads better than warm cream or champagne

Piece 03Silk Blouse · Forest Green or Navy

Structurally identical to Piece 02, in a darker color. The wardrobe plan specifies "navy or forest green button-up blouse, relaxed fit, tuckable." Forest green is the more distinctive aesthetic pick; navy is more conservatively versatile.

If buying only one dark blouse Forest green is harder to replicate elsewhere, more flattering on cool undertones, and anchors the dark-academia/literary register that's central to the capsule. Navy is safer but more replaceable.

Piece 04White Fitted Tee — the Foundation Layer

A quality cotton tee, crew or scoop neck, fitted but not tight. This piece is easy to underrate — a good white tee is the difference between "put together" and "styled" underneath blazers, cardigans, or tucked into high-waist bottoms. Quality matters here precisely because the tee is always partially visible.

What to look for

  • 100% cotton or cotton-modal blend (avoid polyester-dominant)
  • Medium-weight jersey — not see-through, not t-shirt-stiff
  • Crew or scoop neck that sits flat (not loose and stretched-out-looking)
  • Tucked into a high-waist skirt or trouser without pulling out
  • True white or optical white. Not cream or off-white.

Piece 05Cool Pink / Dusty Rose Soft Knit

A crewneck or boatneck knit in a cool pink, dusty rose, or similar cool-toned soft color. This piece exists to keep the palette from becoming monochrome black-and-cream. It should feel soft against the skin and read romantic without veering sweet.

What to look for

  • Color: cool pink (rose with blue undertone), dusty rose, mauve. Avoid peach, coral, or warm pinks that fight cool undertones.
  • Fiber: cashmere, merino, or cotton-cashmere blend preferred. Cotton alone is fine at entry tier.
  • Flat knit (not ribbed — ribbed reads Parisian/body-conscious, which is a different register)
  • Crewneck or boatneck; no V-neck (reads dated in this register)
  • Fitted-but-soft — follows the body without clinging
Flat knit vs. fisherman A fisherman knit reads chunky, thick-textured, and nautical-cottage. Cute in its own register but not this piece. The capsule wants a soft, smooth fine-gauge knit — the same quality of surface as the turtleneck, just a different color and neckline.

Summary

Tops build order

  1. Turtleneck (charcoal) + white tee — Phase 1–2. Foundation.
  2. Cool pink / dusty rose knit — Phase 2–3. Adds color to the palette.
  3. Silk blouse (ivory) — Phase 3–4. The old-money anchor.
  4. Silk blouse (forest green or navy) — Phase 4–5. Darker-register variation.
