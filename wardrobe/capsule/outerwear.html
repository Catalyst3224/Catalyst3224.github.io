Outerwear

Three layers that carry the aesthetic — wool coat, trench, cardigan

Outerwear is where the capsule gets photographed. The wool coat is the piece the synthesized aesthetic specifically points to — "candlelit libraries, autumn afternoons, silk ribbons, wool coats, the feeling of a French film from the 1960s." It's the one piece most responsible for pulling the whole aesthetic together when layered over everything else.

Piece 01Long Wool Coat · Black or Charcoal

A long coat hitting mid-calf — longer than a jacket but shorter than a floor-length duster. Made from wool or wool blend (heavy, warm, structured). Single-breasted (one row of buttons, not two). Clean, minimal silhouette — no belt, no big collars, no fur trim, no hardware details. The shape itself is the statement.

What it's called

  • Long wool coat — generic
  • Overcoat — historical menswear term, increasingly used for women's
  • Maxi coat — marketing term for the long version
  • Car coat — shorter version, usually mid-thigh (not what we want)
  • Duster coat — longer and lighter weight (different piece)

Fabric priority

  1. 100% wool or wool-cashmere blend — the target
  2. Wool-polyester blend (70%+ wool) — acceptable entry tier
  3. Wool-recycled content blends — increasingly common, can be quite good
  4. Polyester-dominant — avoid; doesn't hold shape or warmth the same way

What to look for

  • Length hits mid-calf — test by measuring, product photos lie
  • Single-breasted with clean button line
  • Minimal hardware — horn or matte buttons, nothing shiny
  • Collar sits flat against the coat, not oversized or notched dramatically
  • Lined interior (crucial for wool comfort)
  • Charcoal or black — both work, charcoal is more forgiving in photographs
Fit matters more than brand on this one A $900 wool coat that doesn't fit your shoulders looks worse than a $200 coat that does. Shoulder fit is the one thing tailoring can't cheaply fix. Always order both sizes if borderline and return the worse one — coats especially reward this kind of comparison.

Piece 02Cream or Camel Trench Coat

Classic belted trench, mid-thigh to knee length, lighter weight than the wool coat for spring and autumn. The trench is a specific silhouette — double-breasted, belted at the waist, epaulettes, gun flap, storm shield. These details aren't decoration; they're the visual language of the piece. A "trench" without them is a belted coat.

What to look for

  • Cotton gabardine or cotton-blend — the traditional fabric
  • Double-breasted with 10-button closure
  • Fabric belt (not a separate leather belt)
  • Length hits mid-thigh to knee — longer trenches exist but read different
  • Color: cream, camel, stone, or khaki — classic neutrals only
  • Hardware (buckles, buttons): matte or tortoise — nothing shiny
Cream vs. camel Camel (warm tan) is the classic. Cream (cooler, lighter) works better with cool undertones. For the palette already established, cream is the smarter pick — camel reads warmer and pulls against the rest of the wardrobe.

Piece 03Soft Oversized Cardigan · Cream or Grey

A thigh-length cardigan, oversized in cut (not fitted), in a soft yarn that reads romantic rather than prep. Layers over the slip dress, the nap dress, the silk blouse — and serves as a light outerwear layer in transitional weather when the wool coat is too heavy and the trench is too formal.

What to look for

  • Thigh-length — hits mid-thigh at shortest, longer is fine
  • Oversized cut — drops off the shoulders slightly, relaxed through body
  • Fine-gauge knit, not chunky fisherman (different register)
  • Cashmere, merino, or cotton-cashmere blend preferred
  • Cream, oatmeal, or grey — nothing else; color cardigan is a different piece
  • Plain buttons or hidden placket — no decorative detail

Summary

Outerwear build order

  1. Long wool coat (charcoal) — Phase 2–3. The aesthetic anchor.
  2. Soft oversized cardigan (cream or grey) — Phase 3–4. Transitional and layering.
  3. Trench coat (cream) — Phase 4–5. Spring/autumn, lower urgency than wool coat.
