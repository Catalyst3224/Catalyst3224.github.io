Master Plan/ Personal Care/Nails

Nails

Sunday nail day · Full kit with technique · Cool-toned polish

Weekly Sunday ritual. The current kit is comprehensive — a gift set with both nail tools proper and broader grooming tools. Technique matters as much as the tools: the same cuticle nipper can produce clean results or bleeding fingers depending on how it's used.
Goal

What the routine is for

Healthy nails that grow out intentionally, not accidentally. Cool-toned pink polish on toes from the start; fingers build up slowly — from cuticle care to treatment polish to color to french tips as skill develops. The routine also serves as a weekly anchor — something that consistently happens on Sundays.

Weekly routine

Nail day — Sunday

  1. Soak fingertips in warm soapy water a few minutes (softens cuticles for the pusher and nipper)
  2. Clip straight across if needed (file off more than clip; clipping can split nails)
  3. File in one direction only — no back-and-forth sawing
  4. Push cuticles back with the pusher while still soft
  5. Nip only clearly dead skin (not live cuticle — see technique below)
  6. Buff lightly with buffing block (optional; glossy finish without polish)
  7. Apply Essie Treat Love & Color on fingers (treatment + light color)
  8. Cool-toned pink shade on toes — enjoy it
  9. Cuticle oil on all fingers after polish dries
File direction matters Always file in one direction from edge toward center. Back-and-forth sawing separates the nail layers, which is the primary cause of splitting and peeling. This is the single most repeated mistake in nail care.
The tool kit

Nail-specific tools and technique

Flat Nail Clippers fingernails

Standard clippers with a flat cutting edge. Straight cut across fingernails in small sections rather than one big cut — one clip on each side angling in, then one across the middle. Cutting straight across in one pass can cause splitting. Always clip dry, never after a long soak when nails are soft and prone to tearing.

Diagonal Nail Clippers precision / corners

Angled jaw for getting into corners more precisely. Useful for toenails (where the angle helps visibility) and for detailed corner work on either fingers or toes. Also good for cleaning up a specific corner without repositioning the whole clipper.

Toenail Clippers toenails

Larger and stronger than fingernail clippers. Cut straight across, never round the corners — curved toenail cuts are the primary cause of ingrown toenails. Leave a little white at the edge. Use these instead of finger clippers for feet; the extra leverage matters.

Children's Nail Clippers detail

Smaller jaw for detail work. Useful when the standard clippers feel unwieldy on small nails or for precise corner trimming.

Toe File toenail smoothing

Small file shaped for toenails. File in one direction only. For toes you're mostly smoothing the edge after clipping rather than shaping — straight across is the goal. File lightly rather than rounding corners down significantly.

Nail Picker (angled hook) cleaning under nails

Slightly misnamed — more accurately a cuticle and debris cleaner. The angled tip cleans under the free edge of the nail and along the sides where debris accumulates. Gentle sweeping, not digging. Good after clipping and before polish to ensure clean surface.

Dead Skin Push / Cuticle Pusher critical

One of the most important tools. Pushes back the cuticle (thin skin that grows onto the nail plate from the base) to reveal more of the nail, make it look longer, and prepare the surface for polish.

Technique: do this after soaking when skin is soft — never on dry cuticles (you'll tear). Hold the flat angled end at a low angle (almost flat against the nail, not perpendicular). Small gentle circular motions pushing back toward the base. The goal is to reveal the lunula (half-moon at the base) without exposing raw skin or pain. If it hurts, you're pressing too hard or the skin isn't soft enough.

The pointed or pick end cleans under the nail edge and in the side grooves — same gentle approach.

Dead Skin Pliers / Cuticle Nippers critical — technique-dependent

Used after the pusher. Removes loose/lifted dead skin (hangnails, overgrown cuticle edges) that won't lie flat. Technique matters enormously here — incorrect use causes soreness, bleeding, and worse regrowth.

Technique: only nip skin that is actually loose and lifted. The jaw should close on a clean flap of dead skin with no resistance. If you feel resistance or pulling, stop — you're into live tissue. Small precise snips, repositioning between each.

For hangnails: clip at the base cleanly rather than pulling or tearing (which rips into live skin alongside).

Most common mistake: overcutting. Less is more. You can always take a little more off; you can't put it back.

Double Picks (M & L) detail cleanup

Thin metal picks with a small loop or scoop on one or both ends. M size for fingernails, L for toenails or deeper cleaning. The loop is gentler (sweeping); the pointed end is for precise corner work.

Also useful for cleaning up after polish — the pointed end scrapes off polish on the surrounding skin once dried rather than smudging it wet.

Pedicure Knives — Flat, Bevel, Meniscus, Small callus removal

Callus tools, not nail tools. These are scalpels in different blade shapes for different areas of the foot.

  • Flat pedicure knife — broad flat areas (heel, ball of foot). Low angle, skimming strokes, shaving the surface of hard skin.
  • Bevel pedicure knife — angled blade for contoured areas (side of heel, outer foot curve).
  • Meniscus pedicure knife — curved blade for concave areas (arch, around toe bases).
  • Small knife — detail work, smaller callus patches.

Rules for all of these: softened skin only (after soaking), never dry hard callus. Short light strokes, check progress frequently. Stop when skin looks white or pink — that's live tissue. Dead callus is yellow or grey. Never remove a callus in one session — gradual reduction across multiple pedicures is safer and more effective.

Learning curve These have a real margin for error. If you're new to pedicure work, a pumice stone or foot file is safer while building familiarity. The knives are more efficient but less forgiving.
Acne Needles (straight, hook, double-ended) not nail tools — comedone extraction

Bundled with the kit but outside nail care. Only use on softened pores (after shower or warm compress). The straight needle pierces the surface of a whitehead with one shallow puncture. The loop end expresses the contents with gentle pressure. The hook helps lift blackheads before extraction.

Restraint matters more than technique. Never force anything that isn't ready to come out — that causes scarring and pushed-deeper infections. Clean tool with alcohol before and after. Don't use on inflamed active acne, only closed or surface comedones.

Ear Scoops (M, L, standard) not nail tools — ear wax

Gentle insertion just inside the ear canal opening, light scooping toward the outside. Not deep. The canal is self-cleaning further in, and pushing deep risks impacting wax or damaging the canal. Grooming tool, not a medical one — for significant buildup, ear drops or irrigation are more appropriate.

Eyebrow Clips, Trimmer, Nose Hair Trimmer, Eyelash Comb not nail tools — grooming

Bundled with the kit. The eyebrow clips hold skin taut during precision work. The trimmer scissors cut length on brow hairs that extend beyond the natural line (brush hairs up with the eyelash comb first, then trim strays). Nose hair trimmer is self-explanatory.

Brow shaping (beyond length trimming) is a larger topic — see notes in Makeup on brow shape for the feminine direction. Start conservatively: clean up strays underneath and between the brows only, don't touch the top, until you know the target shape.

Polish progression

What to apply, when

  • Phase 1 — Essie Treat Love & Color on fingers (treatment + light tint). Cool-toned pink on toes — just enjoy it.
  • Phase 2 — Proper cuticle pushing technique added to routine. Begin experimenting with neutral polish on fingers. Expand toe polish collection — build the cool-toned pink range.
  • Phase 3 — Practice clean finger polish application. Expand toe polish with berries and mauves alongside pinks.
  • Phase 4 — French tip practice begins. Essie Blanc for the tips. Use guides or freehand depending on comfort. Consider a UV/LED lamp for longer-lasting results.
  • Phases 5+ — Nail art exploration if desired.
Shade guidance

Cool-toned polish

  • Cool undertone → lean toward blue-based pinks, mauves, raspberries
  • Avoid coral/peach/warm-based pinks
  • Essie shades to explore: Cascade Cool, Maximillian Strasse Her, Sugar Daddy
  • Cuticle oil daily — pairs well with biotin (see Supplements) for nail health
Utility items

Supporting supplies

  • Nail polish remover — necessary. Acetone-based: fast and complete, harsher and drying (follow with cuticle oil). Non-acetone: gentler, slower. Either works.
  • Base coat and top coat — necessary once moving beyond Essie Treat Love (which is all-in-one). Base coat prevents staining; top coat extends wear.
  • Toe spacers — optional but genuinely helpful. Keep toes separated while polish dries. Cheap and reusable.
  • Buffer block — smooths nail surface before polish. Multiple grits per block.
  • Nail file — fine grit for shaping. Glass files last longest.
  • Cuticle oil — already in the daily routine. Any drugstore brand works.
