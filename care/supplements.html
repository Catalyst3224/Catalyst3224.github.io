Master Plan/ Personal Care/Supplements

Supplements

Three daily — biotin, vitamin D, zinc

All with food. All daily. All phases. Simple stack, consistent application — the supplements are additive to skincare, hair, and nail routines rather than a primary driver. Don't skip breakfast just to skip supplements; skip the supplements that day instead.
The stack

What and why

Biotin (vitamin B7)

Purpose: supports hair, skin, and nail growth and strength. Pairs well with the cuticle oil daily habit in the nail routine — biotin works from the inside while the oil works from the outside.

  • Typical dose: 2,500–5,000 mcg daily
  • Take with food
  • Results slow — weeks to months for visible change in nail or hair quality
  • Note: can interfere with certain lab tests (thyroid, troponin). Mention to providers before blood work.

Vitamin D

Purpose: bone health, immune function, mood regulation. Becomes especially relevant with HRT long-term for bone density support. Most people are low even without knowing; Ohio latitudes make deficiency more likely.

  • Typical dose: 1,000–2,000 IU daily (higher if blood work shows deficiency)
  • Take with food — fat-soluble, absorbs better with dietary fat
  • Worth getting baseline level checked at primary care

Zinc

Purpose: immune function, skin health, wound healing. Mild support for dandruff indirectly.

  • Typical dose: 15–30 mg daily
  • Take with food — never on an empty stomach. Zinc on empty stomach causes nausea, sometimes significant.
  • Don't exceed 40 mg/day long-term without reason; excess zinc interferes with copper absorption
The empty-stomach zinc mistake The nausea from zinc on an empty stomach is genuinely unpleasant and can persist for an hour or more. If you take it reliably with food, this is a non-issue. If you forget breakfast on a given morning, skip the zinc rather than taking it alone — it's more memorable than you'd expect.
Not on the list

Supplements not being taken, with reasoning

  • Multivitamin — diminishing returns for most adults with adequate food intake. The three targeted supplements above cover the specific concerns; a general multivitamin adds little.
  • Collagen — evidence is weaker than marketing suggests. If interested, food-based (bone broth, gelatin) is more defensible than powdered supplements.
  • Omega-3 fish oil — worth considering if diet is low in fatty fish, but not a current gap.
  • Iron — only take if blood work shows deficiency. Supplementing iron without need causes constipation and can be harmful.
  • "Hair growth" complexes — usually just biotin with marketing overhead. Skip.
Timing

When in the day

  • With breakfast — all three. Simplest pattern.
  • Pill organizer — Sunday evening as part of week-ahead prep. Removes the decision cost each morning.
  • Skip rather than catch up — if you forget a day, just resume the next day. Don't double up.
