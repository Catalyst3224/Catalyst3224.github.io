Makeup

Theory first, products second — technique built from understanding

Makeup is light and shadow applied to a face. The principles are universal — it's geometry, not gender. What changes between applications is the starting structure: where to highlight, where to contour, where to restrain. Learning the "why" first makes the "how" and "what" much easier.
Goal

What this routine is building toward

Soft, considered, cool-toned makeup that feminizes without costume. Glass-skin base, soft blended eyes, cool-toned lip, subtle cheek work. The aesthetic reference points align with the rest of the plan — Korean/Japanese beauty's emphasis on dewy skin and gradient lips, France Gall's melancholy softness, Ariana Grande's Positions-era soft glam, Ana de Armas's restrained sexy-formal makeup. Not pop glam, not pinup, not contemporary Instagram.

Theory

Light and shadow — Robert Welsh's framework

Makeup works by changing where light and shadow fall on the face, which changes how the eye perceives dimension and proportion. This is universal geometry — the principles don't care about gender or starting face shape. What varies is the goal: someone with a very round face and someone with a very long face use the same tools toward opposite ends.

Practical corollaries:

  • Blush placement changes perceived face shape. Vertical emphasis lengthens; horizontal emphasis widens. Higher on the cheekbone reads lifted and structured; lower is softer but can shorten visually. Toward the ear stays contoured; toward the nose is flushed and youthful but widens.
  • Highlight draws the eye; shadow recedes. Both are tools for the same effect — reshaping perceived structure.
  • Sometimes one tool solves the problem more elegantly than the other. Highlighting down the bridge of the nose creates the same narrowing effect as contouring the sides, and is much more forgiving.

Structural differences and what they mean practically

The principles are identical regardless of face. The application varies because different faces start from different structural baselines. For a typically masculine facial structure — stronger jaw, flatter cheekbones, broader forehead, brow ridge — these are the specific places where intentional work pays off.

Cheekbones first If only one feature gets worked on, make it cheekbones. Low-difficulty application, high visible impact, directly addresses one of the real structural differences. The rest can wait until muscle memory is there.
Color theory

Cool undertones — product guidance

Undertone is confirmed cool. This matters more than shade depth for how makeup reads on skin — a foundation that's the right darkness but wrong temperature makes skin look ashy or sallow, while one with correct temperature works even if darkness is slightly off.

  • Foundation / concealer — labels to look for: rosy, cool, or neutral. "Rosy" = cool in product labeling; they're the same. Avoid golden or warm.
  • Blush — cool pinks, mauves, soft berries. Avoid peach and coral.
  • Eyeshadow — lilac, taupe, cool brown, grey, rose, soft blue. (Modern Lilac Frost by Hindash is a strong inspiration in this register.)
  • Highlight — silver-based or pearl. Not gold or champagne.
  • Bronzer / contour — cool-toned taupe/grey-brown rather than warm orange-brown. Contour should read as shadow, not bronze.
  • Lip — cool pink, mauve, berry. If red, blue-based rather than orange-based.
Nose — highlight over contour Highlighting down the bridge of the nose achieves similar perceived narrowing to contouring the sides, with significantly more forgiveness. Especially for someone learning, bridge highlighting is the better approach. Nose contouring as a technique is lower-priority; bridge highlighting is worth learning earlier than you might otherwise think.
Order of operations

The universal sequence

  1. Skincare — clean face, serum, moisturizer. Glass skin prep is hydration layering before base.
  2. Primer — smooths texture, extends wear
  3. Base — tinted moisturizer / foundation / BB or CC cream
  4. Concealer — under eyes, blemishes, anywhere needing brightness
  5. Setting powder — T-zone only; avoid a full matte face
  6. Cream products next (cream blush, bronzer) — then powder over if desired
  7. Brows
  8. Eyes — shadow first, then liner, then mascara
  9. Lips
  10. Setting spray

Memorable shorthand: skincare → primer → base → eyes → lips → setting. The fuller list above is the reference; this is the working pattern.

Phased build

When to add what

Phase 2 — Research only

  • No products yet. Focus on learning.
  • Watch: Robert Welsh (theory, technique), Alexandra Anele (skincare-makeup overlap), Wayne Goss (professional technique), NikkieTutorials (transferable technique), Shonagh Scott (trans femme application)
  • Identify your eye shape and face shape — affects technique
  • Build a product wishlist based on research
  • Understand order of operations

Phase 3 — Starter kit (build gradually)

Start with just tinted moisturizer and lip balm until comfortable, then add one product at a time.

  1. Primer — Elf Poreless Putty Primer (affordable, excellent)
  2. Tinted moisturizer or light foundation — NARS Tinted Moisturizer or IT Cosmetics CC Cream, cool shade (rosy or neutral undertone)
  3. Concealer — Maybelline Fit Me cool tone, one shade lighter than skin for under eyes
  4. Brow gel — Benefit Gimme Brow, shade matching your hair
  5. Mascara — L'Oreal Telescopic
  6. Tinted lip balm — Laneige Lip Sleeping Mask or similar

Techniques this phase:

  • Glass skin prep — hydration layering before any base products
  • Light hand always — build up rather than applying heavily
  • Focus on skin first, color second

Phase 4 — Eye techniques

  • Tightlining upper waterline — adds definition without obvious makeup look
  • Soft brown or taupe eyeshadow in the crease — adds depth, feminizes eye shape subtly
  • Lower lash line: very light shadow (not liner) — softens and opens eyes
  • Avoid harsh lines — soft and blended always
  • Brow shaping: consider professional threading once to establish shape, maintain at home after

Phase 5 — Color

  • Introduce lip color: cool-toned sheer lipsticks or lip tints first
  • Korean lip tints: Romand or Peripera — affordable, natural, buildable, suit the aesthetic perfectly
  • Gradient / ombre lip technique — darker edges, lighter center (K-beauty standard)
  • Too Faced lip combo — dusty rose lip liner + dusty mauve rose lipstick + lavender-purple gloss with blue pearl shift. Coherent layering within the cool-tone family.
  • Setting spray to make everything last
  • Contour and highlight if desired

Phase 6 — Integrated

  • Full routine internalized
  • Korean beauty techniques fluent — glass skin, gradient lip, drunken blush, inner-corner highlight
  • Hanfu makeup elements to explore later if desired
Brow shaping

High-impact but high-risk

Brow shape significantly affects how feminine or masculine a face reads. Worth doing intentionally, worth doing slowly. For a more feminine brow: higher arch, cleaner edges, slightly thinner but not overplucked. The arch peaks above the outer edge of the iris when looking straight ahead. The tail ends at roughly 45° from the outer corner of the nose through the outer corner of the eye.

Methods

  • Tweezing — most control. One hair at a time. Lasts a couple of weeks. Slow, mildly painful, easy to overdo because micro-decisions lose perspective. Best for maintenance once a shape is established.
  • Razoring / dermaplaning — fast and painless but less precise. Flush with skin rather than at the root, so regrowth is days not weeks. More room for error in one stroke.
  • Scissors — length only, not shape.
  • Threading — cleanest lines, longest results. Requires going somewhere or a real learning curve at home.
Start conservatively Don't touch the top of the brow initially. Only clean up obvious strays underneath and between the brows. This gives a cleaner look with minimal risk of losing your natural shape before the target shape is decided. Once face shape is determined and a target is chosen, tweeze or thread to that target — but don't commit to a shape before knowing the destination. A professional threading session once to establish the shape, then home maintenance, is the safest route.
Resources

Tutorial channels

  • Robert Welsh — highly technical. Best for understanding what products actually do and how light and shadow work. The theory source.
  • Alexandra Anele — skincare-makeup overlap. Good for the base layers.
  • Wayne Goss — professional techniques, has specific feminine / masculine-to-feminine videos.
  • NikkieTutorials — transferable technique. Cut crease, liner, blending — the skill doesn't care about gender.
  • Shonagh Scott — trans femme makeup application specifically. The most directly relevant resource for navigating feminine technique on a face that wasn't "designed" for it.
  • Hindash — technique reference. Genderless application philosophy, excellent work on cool-toned looks (Modern Lilac Frost is the standout). Respect for the work without wanting to look like them.

Shopping

  • Romand, Peripera — Korean lip tints. Affordable, excellent quality.
  • Laneige — Lip Sleeping Mask is a gentle entry into lip product territory.

  • Maybelline Fit Me, L'Oreal Telescopic — reliable drugstore workhorses.
  • NARS, IT Cosmetics — mid-tier foundation/tinted moisturizer with cool shades.

  • Sephora, Ulta — in-person testing when possible; color accuracy across monitors is unreliable.
Utility items

Brushes, sponges, removers

  • Makeup remover — necessary. Garnier Micellar Water (sensitive skin formula) or oil-based cleanser for waterproof products.
  • Cotton rounds — for remover. Bulk pack cheap.
  • Beauty blender or equivalent — necessary for liquid base application. Dampen before use.
  • Brush set — starter kit with powder, blush, eyeshadow (flat packer, blending), and small detail brushes. Real Techniques is the reliable budget option; Morphe for mid-tier.
  • Eyelash curler — optional but high-impact. Use before mascara.
  • Brush cleaner — wash brushes weekly with gentle shampoo or dedicated cleaner. Dirty brushes cause breakouts and muddy colors.
