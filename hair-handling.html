Hair Handling

Tucking, moving, tying, wind management

The gestures people with long hair develop over decades without thinking about them. Once you have hair or a wig to work with, these become practical skills rather than theoretical ones.
Overview

What this subsection covers

The small movements involved in having hair long enough to need managing. Tucking behind the ear, moving it out of the face while eating or bending down, tying it up in various ways, handling wind and weather. Prerequisite: hair long enough, or wig in use.

Prerequisites

Before starting

  • Hair long enough to work with — waits on the hair section progressing, or until a wig is acquired (see Wigs)
  • Hair ties, bobby pins, claw clips available
  • Mirror access for initial practice
Areas

Tucking behind the ear

  • The foundational movement — the little gesture that signals "I have hair that behaves a certain way"
  • Done with one hand, fingers combing through the hair from front to back, tucking strand behind ear
  • Practice in the mirror; video from the front to see what it looks like from outside

Moving hair out of the face

  • When bending down to pick something up — sweep hair back with hand before leaning
  • When eating — tuck or hold back the side closer to the plate
  • When reading or looking down at phone — same gesture

Tying hair up

  • Ponytail — simple, functional. Gather hair back, secure with hair tie, adjust height and tightness.
  • Bun — twist the ponytail and wrap around its base; secure with pins or a bun-specific tie.
  • Half-up — only the top or front portion; leaves the rest loose.
  • All three of these require practice to do without a mirror.

Wind and weather management

  • Walking into wind — shield the face with one hand, or gather hair on one side and hold.
  • Rain or humidity — ponytail preemptively; curl-prone hair benefits from anti-frizz product before outdoor time.
  • Driving with windows down — tie back or accept the aftermath.
Resources

Where to learn

Free

  • YouTube tutorials on basic styles — ponytail, bun, half-up. Look for beginner-level content.
  • Observational practice — notice how women around you handle their hair over the course of normal activities.

Paid

  • Stylist appointment with someone familiar with helping clients with shorter or transitioning hair — can teach styling techniques specific to current length.
Practice structure

How to work on this

  • Build the tuck-behind-ear gesture first — simplest, most frequent movement.
  • Practice ponytails on wig or mannequin head first if hair isn't long enough yet.
  • One style per week — ponytail, then bun, then half-up. Don't try all at once.
  • Don't overdo the touch-gestures — moderation matters; constant hair-fiddling reads anxious rather than feminine.
Milestones

Using the five-stage framework

Each area moves through Introduced → Deliberate → Supported → Default → Integrated independently.

  • Phase 3–4 — With shoulder-length hair or wig, tucking and moving-out-of-face gestures become practical.
  • Phase 4–5 — Tying up — ponytail, bun, simple braids.
  • Phase 5–6 — Full styling with length, plus wind and weather management becoming automatic.
